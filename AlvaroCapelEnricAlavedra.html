1) If we propose an ARMA(2,0) model and an ARMA(1,1), the second is a better model.

Answer: FALSE. The comparison has to be based on AIC and BIC as both models have the same number of parameters. The ARMA(2,0) achieves a little bit lower values for both AIC and BIC, meaning it fits the data better with equal complexity. Nevertheless, we consider it is not enough to confirm ourt decision and that is why we have used the Ljung-Box test to confirm that residuals of both models have equall noise (that is, equall complexity). As confirmed, both models can be compared without missing information.

6) The once-differenced (d=1) log-transformed-series has a non-significant mean. Thus, when fitting an AR(1) process, it is always better to drop this parameter (intercept) from the model.

Answer: FALSE. In this question, two key points are studied: at first, checking if the mean of the differenced log-series it is significant: having a t-test p-value > 0.05, the intercept may be non-significant. But, as we consider it is not enough for a robust justification, we are checking information criteria for both models: the one considering the intercept and the one without considering the mean. Conclusions are not easy to derive, as the AIC of the model not considering the mean is lower but it is not the case for BIC. As a conclusion, we consider the statement it is false due to the word “always” as we cannot afirm that.

7) When fitting an ARIMA(0, 2, 5) model to the original series, the residuals are reasonably well behaved and thus we validate the model

Answer: FALSE. As we did in Q6, if information criteria is used as a metric then we can say that having higher values for the new fitting means a worse fit despite more parameters. As a final confirmation, we have made a Box-Ljung test to verify, by p-value, that indeed the reference model performs much better than new model proposal.

Moreover, just looking at the different tests for the residuals of the ARIMA(0, 2, 5) model we can conclude: Independence looks fine: Durbin–Watson p-value=0.2382 and all Ljung–Box p-values > 0.05, so there is no evidence of residual autocorrelation. But normality clearly fails: Shapiro–Wilk, Anderson–Darling and Jarque–Bera all with p-values < 0.05, so we can reject the null hypothesis of normality.

And homoscedasticity also fails: Breusch–Pagan: p=0.02241<0.05

So the residuals are not reasonably well behaved overall. They are independent, but not normal and not homoscedastic, hence the model is not validated
